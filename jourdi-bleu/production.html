Brand Voice Guide

The brand should sound like she actually talks.

This guide was built from four sources: the discovery conversation transcript, the ShoutOut Arizona press feature, the speaking page copy (her strongest public-facing writing), and the breathwork page. The goal is to close the gap between the woman in conversation and the brand on the website.

Five words that define how Jourdi sounds

Raw She shares the real version of her story — not the polished highlight reel. The struggle is the credibility.
Direct Speaks at one person, not an audience. 'You' not 'women' or 'clients.' Short declarative sentences land alongside longer flowing ones.
Warm Genuine care, not performative positivity. No toxic optimism. She can say hard things because the warmth underneath them is real.
Light Takes serious things seriously but not solemnly. Irreverence about spirituality makes the depth feel approachable — not scary, not exclusive.
Responsible Owns her claims. 'I say that with deep responsibility.' The boldness is earned, not borrowed. She doesn't overclaim and she doesn't shrink.

How she builds a sentence

Short-then-long rhythm

She hits hard with a short sentence. Then she opens into the explanation — often with a dash or a colon. The rhythm breathes like the work itself: quick compression, then release.

"You can't think your way out of it. The breath goes where words have never been — into the body, into what's been waiting."

Second person, present tense

Always "you," not "women" or "clients." Always present tense — "you feel" not "you will feel." This makes the reader feel seen right now, not promised something later.

"You already have the answers. You just need somewhere safe to hear them."

Rhetorical questions as pivots

She uses questions to shift perspective — not as a marketing device, but as the actual mechanism of her coaching. The question is the offer.

"What if you stopped trying to fix that part of you — and just loved her instead?"

Personal testimony as proof

"When I..." and "In my experience..." are not overshares — they're the whole point. Her story is the product demonstration. She earned her credibility the hard way.

"I used to have insane stomach pain. No doctor could explain it. It was gone after the breathwork."

Duality & both/and framing

She doesn't flatten complexity. "Both can be true." "You can not like a part of yourself and still have compassion for it." This is her philosophy in sentence form.

"It's intense and it's gentle. You're in charge and it will take you somewhere you didn't expect."

Spiritual language — earned, not decorative

"Upstairs Self," "energy," "portal," "the Universe" — these aren't buzzwords for her. They're the operating system. Use them, but ground them in what they actually mean in practice.

"Your Upstairs Self — the part of you beyond the noise, beyond the story — knows exactly what to do."

The language map

✓ Say this

  • "You already have the answers" — her core belief, say it often
  • "Your Upstairs Self" — own this term, use it consistently
  • "Feel it for yourself" — the product promise, keep it front and centre
  • "It lives in your body" — her key reframe, use it to contrast with talk therapy
  • "Choose your own adventure" — signals safety and agency to her audience
  • "Both can be true" — her philosophy in four words
  • "The real work" — what she offers, vs. surface-level wellness
  • "Lighter" — the most common outcome word her clients use
  • Em dashes — she thinks in dashes; the copy should too
  • Short + long sentence rhythm — compress, then release

× Avoid this

  • "Your most radiant self" — generic, could be anyone's tagline
  • "Empower/empowerment" — overused in the wellness space, no texture
  • "On a journey" — cliché, say what the journey actually is
  • "It's time to..." — used by every coach on Instagram
  • "Transform your life" — too vague, no felt sense
  • "Holistic wellness" — not her lane, too broad
  • Passive voice — she's direct; the copy should be too
  • Overly polished, distance-creating language — this brand lives close
  • Sentence structures that speak to "women" as a demographic group
  • Promising outcomes without the "but it takes practice" caveat
