Brand Architecture Audit

The brand is ahead of the website. That's the assignment.

Function + beauty = the real magic.

Archive doesn't have a website yet — and that's actually the cleanest starting position possible. There's no wrong impression to undo, no outdated copy to apologize for. There's just Stephanie: credentialed, confident, technically trained, with a live project at $125/hr already in motion and a builder ready to refer her to every client he has.

The task is not to explain what an interior designer does. The task is to build a front door that looks exactly as good as the rooms she's about to fill — and signals, in seconds, that Archive is not the typical interior design website. Because it isn't. And she isn't.

The five brand anchors

Collected. Warm. Layered. Storied. Unapologetic.
  • Collected

    Every object has a story. Archive isn't decorated — it's curated. Spaces feel found, not fabricated. The brand communicates that same intentionality: nothing is filler, everything earns its place.

  • Warm

    Terracotta, rust, ochre, amber. Warmth as a design value, not a trend. Cool tones are almost entirely absent from the visual world Archive lives in. The brand should feel like a well-lit room, not a showroom.

  • Layered

    Texture is the hero — woven baskets, stacked books, kilim rugs, linen on linen. The brand communicates depth, not surface. A client who works with Archive doesn't get a room. They get an experience that builds over time.

  • Storied

    Archive means "a place for important, special memories, feelings, and moments kept safe." The word does work. Every room Stephanie designs is meant to hold something. Copy and positioning should carry that weight.

  • Unapologetic

    Stephanie is an interior architect in a market that calls her a decorator. She trained for this. She has a 4.0. She's already charging $125/hr. The brand should wear its confidence the same way she does — warm but completely sure of itself.

Top 5 website build priorities

  • Signal taste before anything else

    The homepage above the fold has one job: prove this is a different kind of interior design studio. Editorial header image, wide typography, warm and textural. If it looks like every other ID website, the brand has already failed at the front door.

  • Lead with the credential, not just the service

    "Interior architect" should appear in the first two scrolls of the homepage — not buried in an About page. This is the thing that earns the rate, earns the referral, and earns the trust of clients who've been burned before.

  • Make the process feel like care, not a checklist

    Stephanie's process is sensory and empathy-driven: mapping aversions, listening deeply, asking who visits the space and what makes them feel safe. That process needs a home on the site — because it's what high-end clients are actually buying.

  • Launch with portfolio content — even one project

    Before/after from the Buffalo Mountain store project, plus documentation from the OmniBuild cabin as it progresses. One beautifully photographed project tells the story better than any copy can. Budget for the $4,000 shoot when the cabin is done.

  • SEO-anchor around "interior designer Floyd VA" and surrounding region

    Floyd is small. Roanoke, Blacksburg, Christiansburg, and the New River Valley are the radius. Affluent clients moving to the Blue Ridge from out-of-state search nationally, then locally. Own the local terms before any competitor does.

The service architecture

Entry The Intro

Discovery call (free). Visual style quiz (Stephanie's own action item — not yet live).

30-min discovery call, no charge. Add the visual style quiz before launch — it does double duty as a lead qualifier and a client onboarding tool. Low friction, high signal.

Standalone The Consult

Not clearly defined as a standalone paid offer yet.

Design Consultation — flat fee $250–$350, 90 min. For clients who need direction but aren't ready for a full project. Generates immediate revenue, builds relationship, often converts to full-service. Good for the "can you just tell me what to do with this room" client.

Core Design Only

Concept development, mood boards → presentation boards, to-scale furniture plans, construction documents. Hourly at $125+.

Keep hourly model. On the website: describe this as concept through construction documents — what the client gets, why it matters, what would happen without it. Emphasize technical depth (real drawings, not Pinterest boards).

Add-On FF&E Procurement

Furniture, fixtures & equipment selection + purchasing. Hourly + markups (trade pricing to retail differential).

Keep the model. On the website: emphasize access — trade pricing, vendor relationships, quality control. Clients don't just save time. They get things they can't get themselves. The markup conversation stays off the website.

Premium Full Service

Design + FF&E + project management. OmniBuild model ($125/hr confirmed).

This is the flagship. Lead with it on the services page. For the "Sweetheart Fancy Pants" client — VP, doctor, person who values time above all — full service means one person who handles everything and reports back. That's the sell.

B2B Builder Partnership

OmniBuild anchor relationship. Matt referring all clients to Stephanie.

Consider a quiet "For Builders" section or page — not public-facing but linkable for referral conversations. Positions Archive as a builder-integrated studio that understands job site dynamics and respects the contractor relationship. Long-term this is a major growth lever.

Future Retainer

Considering for ongoing client relationships. Not yet defined or offered.

Once two or three full-service projects are complete, introduce a Retainer option — monthly design direction for existing clients (style quiz updates, seasonal refreshes, new room additions). Passive-ish recurring income. Archive Seasonal is already in the 5-year vision — this is the precursor to it.

Avatar → Build · Conversion Strategy

The website's only job: turn a warm referral into a booked call.

Archive's primary acquisition channel is referral — OmniBuild, word-of-mouth, the growing network of people who know Stephanie. The website's job is not to generate cold leads. It's to be the place a warm referral lands that makes them think "yes, obviously, I'm booking this call." Every page decision flows from that.

The referral arrival journey

Step 1 — They arrive

Homepage above the fold

What they need to feel in 5 seconds: This designer has taste. This isn't like every other interior design site. She's serious. They load the page and something about the visual language tells them immediately — this is different.

Build decision: Hero image must be editorial and textural, not airy and white. Wide/compressed typography (Canva equivalent of Portal). Warm color palette. No stock-looking imagery.

Step 2 — They scroll

Credential + philosophy signal

What they need to know: She's not a decorator. She's trained as an interior architect. She understands the technical side and the human side — and she "speaks builder" so the project won't turn into a job site power struggle.

Build decision: Short credential callout within first two scrolls. Not a resume — a positioning statement. "Interior architect. Not just interior designer."

Step 3 — They investigate

About page and/or Work page

What they're looking for: Proof that the taste level is real. A person behind the brand they can trust. The story behind the name "Archive." Something that makes them feel like this designer would understand their specific project.

Build decision: About page leads with the Archive concept before credentials. Work page needs at least one fully documented project — even one good project is enough to establish the eye.

Step 4 — They consider

Services + Process pages

What they need to see: Options that feel tailored to them (a la carte model). A process that sounds thorough but human. Honest timeline expectations ("things take longer — she'll tell you upfront, and that's actually a good sign").

Build decision: Services page is clear but warm. Process page shows empathy before it shows phases. The nervous system reset language belongs here — this is what the process is designed to produce.

Step 5 — They convert

Contact / Start Here page

What they need to feel: Low friction. Clear next step. A sense that the discovery call is a conversation, not a pitch. The visual style quiz (once built) gives them something to do that feels useful before the call.

Build decision: Single clear CTA. Simple form. "Here's what happens next" — three sentences. Book via TidyCal or similar. No pricing on this page.

The visual brand signals that do the conversion work

Signal What it communicates How to build it in Canva

Warm, textural imagery This designer understands how rooms feel, not just how they look. No stock white-room photography. Use imagery with warmth — terracotta, linen, woven materials. Even Canva stock has better options if filtered correctly.
Monotone with intentional color pops Restraint = taste. One intentional pop of color per view signals a designer who edits. Dark base, cream text, one warm accent color (rust/terracotta) used sparingly and with intention.

No clip art, no busy graphics "THIS IS NOT AI" energy. Everything is deliberate. Graphic elements should feel architectural — thin rules, structured grids, no decorative ornaments that could come from a template.
