Brand Voice Guide

The brand should sound like someone who knows exactly what she loves.

This guide was built from the discovery call transcript, Stephanie's own words about her aesthetic influences, and the brand identity work completed in the branding session. The goal: close any gap between the woman in conversation — direct, confident, warm, unhurried — and the copy that a potential client reads before they've ever met her.

Five words that define how Archive sounds

Directorial Stephanie self-identifies as interior designer AND creative director. The copy should carry that dual identity — not asking permission, not hedging. It directs.
Warm There's real care for the client's lived experience. "Sweetheart Fancy Pants" — Kyra's term for the ideal Archive client — captures it perfectly: tasteful, resourceful, time-poor, and high-standard. The brand is warm without being soft.
Unhurried Projects take 6 months to a year. She won't rush it. The brand doesn't hustle — it takes its time because time is how you get it right. The copy breathes at the same pace the work does.
Credentialed Radford BA, 4.0 GPA, trained as interior architect. Understands Kelvins, universal design, construction documents. She earned the right to speak with authority — the copy should use it.
Honest "Things will go wrong. I'll tell you upfront." Stephanie is radically honest about timelines and expectations — and that honesty is a competitive advantage. The copy doesn't oversell. It sets the table accurately.

How Archive builds a sentence

Short-then-open rhythm

Leads with a crisp declarative. Then opens into the explanation. The rhythm mirrors the work itself: precise intent, then generous execution.

"This is not a decoration service. It's a complete rethinking of how your space works for your life."

Sensory before technical

Lead with how a space will feel — exhale, warmth, the smell of linen — then follow with the technical credibility that earns it. Never lead with the credential. Lead with the experience the credential makes possible.

"The room you've been trying to create for years — we'll find it. Not by following trends, but by listening to you."

Curation language, not decoration language

"Curated" over "decorated." "Designed" over "styled." "Collected" over "purchased." The vocabulary signals the level of intentionality before the client even reads the description.

"We don't fill rooms. We build them — layer by layer, object by object, texture by texture."

Second person, present tense

"You" not "clients." "Your home" not "the home." Present tense — "you feel" not "you will feel." The reader should feel seen in real time, not promised something in the future.

"You already know what you love. Archive helps you trust it — and build around it."

The honest caveat as credibility signal

Stephanie's radical honesty about timelines and what goes wrong in renovation is a differentiator. Lean into it. It signals experience, not uncertainty — clients have been burned by designers who promised what they couldn't deliver.

"Projects like this take time — usually more than you expect. That's normal. And I'll tell you exactly why at every step."

Builder-bridge language

For contractor referral contexts and the About page: "I speak builder" is a real differentiator. Name it without jargon. Clients who've had communication failures on a job site will feel it immediately.

"I trained as an interior architect. I understand what the contractor needs, what the architect drew, and what you actually want — and I translate between all three."

The language map

✓ Archive says this

  • "Curated" — central to the brand concept; use it often and mean it
  • "Interior architect" — the credential that separates her from every decorator in the region
  • "The space works for your life" — outcome language, not aesthetic language
  • "Collected over time" — signals the unhurried, intentional quality of the work
  • "The rooms your life actually happens in" — grounds the work in lived experience
  • "Function + beauty" — Stephanie's own words; this is the core philosophy
  • "We listen before we design" — the empathy differentiator, up front
  • Sensory words: texture, warmth, exhale, light, layered, unhurried
  • Em dashes — confident punctuation; the copy thinks in dashes
  • Archive, Archive Home, Archive as concept — build the brand vocabulary

× Archive avoids this

  • "Dream home" — overused in the design space, no texture
  • "Transform your space" — every design website says this
  • "Elevate your home" — meaningless without context
  • "Luxury" as a standalone word — show it, don't claim it
  • "Innovative design solutions" — corporate language, wrong register
  • "We're passionate about design" — every competitor says this
  • "Timeless and modern" — this phrase has been used to death
  • Passive voice — Archive is direct; the copy should be too
  • AI-sounding, overly polished language — Stephanie literally wrote "THIS IS NOT AI" on her brand board
  • Any copy that could belong to any other interior design studio in the country

Process Intro

Great design doesn't start with a mood board. It starts with questions — the kind most designers don't ask. Who visits your home? What makes you feel safe in a room? What do you absolutely hate? What did you love about the last space you lived in?

Archive's process is circular, not linear. We design, review, adjust, and iterate — not because we didn't get it right the first time, but because great rooms evolve. Here's how it works.

Phase 1 — Discovery

01 · Discovery
We start with a conversation. What's the space? Who lives in it? What's working, what isn't, and — importantly — what do you hate? (Aversions are as important as preferences.) We use a visual style quiz and Pinterest boards to build a shared language before anyone touches a drawing.

Phase 2 — Vision

02 · Vision
Mood boards — not one, usually several — that explore the full range of where the space could go. These are exploratory and collage-style: textures, palettes, references, objects. This is where we see what resonates, what doesn't, and why.

Phase 3 — Design Development

03 · Design Development
From mood board to presentation board — the refined direction, this is what we're doing. To-scale furniture plans. Construction drawings. Material and finish specifications. Shop drawings for custom elements, with revision rounds built into the process.

Phase 4 — Procurement

04 · Procurement
Selecting, sourcing, and ordering everything the space needs. Trade vendor relationships, quality control, delivery coordination. This phase takes as long as it takes — good pieces have lead times, and we won't rush them.

Phase 5 — Installation + Reveal

05 · Installation
The day the room comes together. We oversee placement, styling, and any final adjustments. You walk back in — and the space you imagined is the space you're standing in.

Timeline Honesty Block

A note on timing.
Renovation and design projects almost always take longer than you expect. A typical full-service project runs 6 months to a year — sometimes more. That's not a warning sign. It's what quality takes.

Stephanie will tell you the real timeline from the first call. She'd rather you know up front than be surprised mid-project.

This block is a differentiator. Leave it in. It builds trust and pre-qualifies clients who need a quick turnaround.
