You said it yourself: you're not selling a look. You're selling deep listening, clarity, and a design process that starts with the people who actually live there. That's what the copy needs to say.

Studio management platform for proposals, contracts, invoicing, and client portals — all in one. Showit is the standard for design-forward service businesses. Plan the migration before you invest more into the current Canva build.

The goal of the back end: a client who finds Archive through a referral should be able to go from discovery to signed contract without anything feeling informal or DIY.

rX

Do these 6 things. In this order:

1

Open the business bank account and set up Stripe before you take your first paid project

You're already mid-launch and the financial infrastructure is the one thing that has to happen before anything else. Venmo and Zelle work for splitting dinner — not for four-figure design retainers. Get the bank account open, connect it to Stripe, and issue your first invoice through a tool that looks like a business. This is a one-day task with a disproportionate credibility return.

2

Solve the portfolio problem now — before you go fully live

You don't need client photos to have a portfolio. You have conceptual work from school and your own home. Curate 3–5 tight case study entries: a concept board, a materials palette, a short process note about what you were solving for, and one strong final image. Label them clearly — "Personal residence," "Academic project." Clients hiring at this level respect transparency about process. What they won't respect is nothing. The Archives page needs content before anyone is referred to your site.

3

Set your rate before you take your first inquiry

Pricing isn't on the site — that's appropriate for a bespoke service. But you need to know your number before a client asks. Residential interior designers typically work on hourly ($85–200/hr), flat project fee, or a percentage of project budget (10–25%). Pick a structure, set a floor, and know it cold before your first consultation. Underpricing in the first six months sets a baseline you'll spend years correcting.

4

Update the homepage headline to make your differentiation explicit

Replace "Creating spaces that feel intuitive, and support the way people live, work, and gather" with something that says what makes you different — not just what design does in general. Your positioning sentence from Section 1 is already written. Put it on the page. A referral arriving at your site should understand immediately why you specifically, not just what interior designers do in general.

5

Plan the migration from Canva to Squarespace or Showit — don't invest more in the current build

Canva served you well for launch speed, but it's a ceiling, not a foundation. Squarespace offers the best balance of SEO infrastructure, native inquiry forms, and design control for a service business at your stage. Showit is better for design-forward practices with more time investment. Pick one, plan the migration, and stop adding features to the Canva site in the meantime.

6

Add a simple email opt-in and claim your Google Business Profile

Two low-effort, high-return moves: (1) add a one-field email opt-in — even "occasional notes from the studio" is enough for people who aren't ready to hire yet; (2) claim your Google Business Profile with your location, category (interior designer), and 2–3 photos. Neither replaces referral strategy — but they're the free credibility signals a $10K/project client will quietly check after they've been referred.
