Who She Is.
What to Say.

01 — Avatar

Who You're Talking To

A wellness practitioner — bodyworker, astrologer, somatic guide, herbalist, movement teacher. Serious work. Real clients. Sustained by word of mouth because the work speaks for itself.

But her digital presence doesn't match. Her site is a rough draft of who she was two years ago. Her offers live in her head. Her brand was built in a weekend and never revisited.

She's not here because she found you on Instagram. Someone she respects said your name. She's already halfway convinced. She just needs to know you understand work that doesn't fit a template.

The gap isn't between her and her audience. It's between who she is and what her business currently shows the world.
02 — Internal World

What She's Carrying

The core tension

"I do serious work. Nothing I've tried to build around it reflects that."

What she actually wants

  • To stop re-explaining herself to every new person
  • To raise her rates without having to justify them
  • To feel proud when someone Googles her — not anxious
  • A business that runs when she's tired

What she's afraid of

  • Building something that flattens or cheapens the work
  • Handing it to someone who doesn't understand what she does
  • Spending money on something that looks like everyone else
  • Getting locked into a direction she'll outgrow

Why she stalls

She's tried fixing this herself. It didn't land. She's not unmotivated — she's burned by iteration that didn't work. She needs someone who leads. Not someone who asks her to figure it out first.

She doesn't need convincing. She needs containment.
03 — Axioms

The Truths You Repeat

These aren't taglines. They're accurate statements that earn trust on contact. Use them across posts, captions, site copy, DMs.

Some work can't be niched down. It has to be architected.
The website isn't the problem. It's where the problem becomes visible.
Word of mouth keeps you alive. A brand keeps you from starting over every season.
You don't need to show up more. You need a presence that works when you don't.
Clarity isn't something you find. It's something you build structure around.
The brand has to hold the ask before you make it.
04 — Content Pillars

Five Things to Post About

Rotate between these. Don't live in one. Each maps to a real state she's in right now.

01
The Diagnosis

Name the real problem before she does

She thinks it's aesthetic. It's structural. Say that clearly, without blame.

Hook

"If you've rewritten your bio three times and it still doesn't feel right — that's not a writing problem."

Caption

"Most practitioners don't have a brand problem. They have a clarity problem that shows up in the brand."

Opener

"The website isn't the issue. The website is just where the issue becomes visible."

02
The Container

Show her you lead — she doesn't have to figure it out first

Her fear is managing the process, not the outcome. Show her there's no sprawl.

Process post

"Clients don't need to know what they want the site to look like. They need to tell me what the work actually does. I take it from there."

Story fragment

"She came in with three different directions and a Canva folder full of midnight logos. We didn't use any of it. By week two she had something that finally felt like her."

03
The Uncategorizable

Speak directly to work that resists easy description

This is your white space. Nobody else is saying this. It will stop the scroll.

Identity post

"Some work sits at the intersection of three things that have no shared category. That's not a marketing problem. That's just what it means to do original work. The brand has to be built differently."

Short hook

"Not every practice fits a template. Some require architecture."

04
The Gap

Make the distance between her work and her presence visible — without shame

She already knows. She's embarrassed. Normalize it. Then orient her toward the fix.

Normalizing post

"The practitioners doing the most serious work are often the ones with the most outdated presence. Not because they don't care. Because nothing available quite fits."

Direct observation

"If you feel anxious when someone Googles you — not because you're hiding something, but because what they'll find doesn't represent you anymore — that's worth closing."

05
The After

Show the quiet, specific relief — no hype

Not transformation. Not "dream life." Grounded scenes. One real moment.

Client moment

"She sent me the link with no message. Just the link. I knew what that meant."

Result, no numbers

"Six weeks after we finished, she raised her rates. Not because I told her to — because the brand finally held the ask."

The baseline

"You send someone to your website and you're not nervous about what they'll find. That's what we're building toward."

05 — Voice

How to Write Every Post

Never

  • "Show up authentically"
  • "Dream clients"
  • "Scale your impact"
  • "You deserve this"
  • "Align your brand with your values"
  • Exclamation points
  • Emoji as decoration
  • Vague empowerment language

Always

  • Write to one real person
  • Name it before she does
  • Short sentences over long ones
  • Facts carry feeling — no extra coating
  • Calm tone, clear point
  • Specific over general, always
  • Say less than you think you need to
06 — Platform

Where & How

Instagram is your referral closer — not your discovery engine

She's not scrolling looking for a brand strategist. Someone already told her your name. She's on your profile deciding if you understand her world. Every post is a trust signal for someone already halfway there.

Format priority

Carousels for diagnosis + process.
Single image for axioms + observations.
Reels only when grounded — no trending audio dependency.

Cadence

3–4x per week. Consistency beats volume. One CTA per week max — make it a reply, not a link chase.

Content split

60% — Diagnosis / observation, no pitch
25% — Process / how you work
15% — Soft invitation
Never lead with the offer. Earn it first.

Monthly must-have

One post that names your client type so specifically a past client could forward it and say "this is what I meant." Highest ROI post you'll write.

07 — Emotional Levers

Moments That Move Her

Raw material for posts, carousels, captions. Each one is a specific scene — not a concept.

What gets better

  • She sends someone to her website and feels proud, not apologetic
  • A new referral arrives already oriented — they read the site first
  • She raises her rates and the brand carries the ask
  • She stops rewriting her bio. It's done. It says the right thing
  • A practitioner she respects asks who built her site

What goes away

  • No longer anxious when someone Googles her
  • No longer re-explaining what she does to every new person
  • No longer holding her entire offer structure in her head
  • No longer making herself smaller to fit a template that wasn't built for her

What keeps getting worse if she waits

  • Another year of referrals landing on a website that doesn't close the loop
  • Watching peers with less depth build better presence — and get the inquiries
  • Word of mouth keeps her afloat. One slow season and there's no foundation
  • The gap between her work and her presence quietly widens as the work deepens

What she never gets to feel if she stays stuck

  • The relief of a business that reflects who she actually is
  • Clients arriving already trusting — before the first session
  • Feeling legitimate in the business layer — not just in the room with a client
08 — White Space

What Nobody Else Is Saying

This is your competitive advantage. Most brand people talk to founders (too corporate) or creatives (too aesthetic-obsessed). Nobody is speaking to the serious practitioner whose work resists packaging.

She's hearing:

  • "Just niche down"
  • "Tell your story"
  • "Show up consistently"

She needs to hear:

  • "Some work can't be niched. It has to be architected."
  • "The brand isn't a story. It's a container for the work."
  • "You don't need to show up more. You need a presence that works when you don't."
Your competitor isn't another brand strategist. It's her continued tolerance of a business that underrepresents her.
